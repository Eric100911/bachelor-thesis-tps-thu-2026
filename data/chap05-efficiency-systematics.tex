\section{Signal Extraction from the Three-Dimensional Mass Spectrum}
\label{sec:signal-extraction}

The signal yield is extracted from the selected data sample of $48\,829$ events by an extended unbinned maximum likelihood (UML) fit to the two dimuon invariant masses $m(J/\psi_1)$, $m(J/\psi_2)$, and the dikaon invariant mass $m(\phi)$. The fit is performed simultaneously in three dimensions using the \textsc{RooFit} framework~\cite{Verkerke:2003ir}.

Each $J/\psi \to \mu^+\mu^-$ resonance is described by a double-sided Crystal Ball (DSCB) function~\cite{Oreglia:1980cs}. The tail parameters $\alpha_L$, $\alpha_R$, $n_L$, and $n_R$ are shared between the two $J/\psi$ mesons, reflecting the common detector response; the mean $\mu$ and resolution $\sigma$ float independently for each. The $\phi \to K^+K^-$ resonance is modelled by a Voigtian function --- a convolution of a relativistic Breit--Wigner with a Gaussian resolution kernel --- with the Breit--Wigner width fixed to the PDG value $\Gamma_\phi = 4.249\;\mathrm{MeV}$~\cite{PDG2024}.

The combinatorial background in each $J/\psi$ mass distribution is modelled by an exponential function with an independent slope for each $J/\psi$. The $\phi$ background is described by a threshold power-law times an exponential, $(m - m_{\mathrm{thr}})^p \cdot \exp(a_1(m - m_{\mathrm{thr}}))$, with the $K^+K^-$ kinematic threshold fixed at $m_{\mathrm{thr}} = 0.987354\;\mathrm{GeV}$.

Denoting the signal PDF as \textbf{S} and the background PDF as \textbf{B}, the eight possible combinations of one $J/\psi_1$ PDF, one $J/\psi_2$ PDF, and one $\phi$ PDF are constructed as independent fit components. This accounts for all ways in which genuine resonances and combinatorial backgrounds can combine in a three-body final state:

The statistical significance of the $J/\psi J/\psi \phi$ signal is evaluated with a likelihood ratio test. The null hypothesis, in which the signal yield $N_{sss}$ is fixed to zero, is refitted. The test statistic $q_0 = -2\ln(\mathcal{L}_{N_{sss}=0} / \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{best}})$ follows a half-$\chi^2(1)$ distribution under Wilks' theorem~\cite{Wilks:1938}, giving $Z = \sqrt{q_0}$. The null-hypothesis fit yields $\mathrm{NLL} = -732\,524$, giving

\begin{equation}
Z = \sqrt{144.2} = 12.0 .
\end{equation}

This result far exceeds the conventional $5\,\sigma$ threshold for discovery, providing unambiguous evidence for the existence of the $J/\psi J/\psi \phi$ final state in proton-proton collisions at $\sqrt{s} = 13.6 \mathrm{TeV}$.

\section{Acceptance Correction}
\label{sec:acceptance-correction}

The acceptance quantifies the probability that the decay products of a meson reach the sensitive volume of the detector and leave reconstructable signals. For the muons from $J/\psi \to \mu^+\mu^-$, the single-muon fiducial requirements are
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:muon-fiducial}
    p_{\mathrm{T}}(\mu^{\pm}) > 3.5\;\mathrm{GeV},
  \qquad
    |\eta(\mu^{\pm})| < 2.4 .
\end{equation}
These thresholds are standard in CMS quarkonium analyses: the $p_{\mathrm{T}}$ cut ensures that muons traverse enough muon stations to be reconstructed with high efficiency, while the $\eta$ requirement restricts muons to the geometrical coverage of the muon system~\cite{CMS_MUON_PERF_13TEV}. For the kaons from $\phi \to K^+K^-$, the fiducial criteria follow the charged-track acceptance of the silicon tracker:
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:kaon-fiducial}
    p_{\mathrm{T}}(K^{\pm}) > 0.5\;\mathrm{GeV},
  \qquad
    |\eta(K^{\pm})| < 2.4 .
\end{equation}

The acceptance is computed per object --- separately for the leading $J/\psi$, the subleading $J/\psi$, and the $\phi$ meson --- as a function of the meson's transverse momentum $p_{\mathrm{T}}$ and absolute rapidity $|y|$. For a given $(p_{\mathrm{T}}, |y|)$ bin, the per-object acceptance is defined as the fraction of generated mesons in that bin for which both decay daughters satisfy the fiducial criteria of Eqs.~\eqref{eq:muon-fiducial}--\eqref{eq:kaon-fiducial}. This is determined using a generator-level-only MC sample, since no detector simulation is needed.

The total acceptance for a $J/\psi J/\psi \phi$ event is approximated by the product of the three per-object acceptances:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:acceptance-product}
A_{\mathrm{tot}}\bigl(\vec{x}_{J/\psi_1}, \vec{x}_{J/\psi_2}, \vec{x}_{\phi}\bigr)
\;\approx\;
A_{J/\psi}\bigl(\vec{x}_{J/\psi_1}\bigr)\;
A_{J/\psi}\bigl(\vec{x}_{J/\psi_2}\bigr)\;
A_{\phi}\bigl(\vec{x}_{\phi}\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\vec{x} = (p_{\mathrm{T}}, |y|)$ denotes the kinematic coordinates of each meson. This factorisation assumes that the decay kinematics of each meson are independent of the others, which is valid for unpolarised $J/\psi$ production and is a standard treatment in CMS multiple-quarkonium measurements~\cite{CMS_TRI_JPSI}. The same per-object acceptance map is used for both $J/\psi$ mesons, justified by the fact that the acceptance depends on the decay properties of the meson rather than its rank ordering in $p_{\mathrm{T}}$.

The per-object acceptance maps are shown in Figs.~\ref{fig:acc-jpsi-lead}--\ref{fig:acc-phi}. The acceptance is binned in
\[
p_{\mathrm{T}} \in [6,10,15,20,30,50,100]\;\mathrm{GeV},
\qquad
|y| \in [0,\;0.6,\;1.2,\;1.8,\;2.4].
\]
At low $p_{\mathrm{T}}$ and high $|y|$, the acceptance drops because one or both decay muons fall outside the fiducial $\eta$ range. At high $p_{\mathrm{T}}$, the decay products are more collimated along the meson direction, and the acceptance approaches unity across all rapidity bins.

\section{Efficiency Correction}
\label{sec:efficiency-correction}

Once the decay products are within the detector acceptance, they must be reconstructed by the CMS tracking and muon systems, identified as signal candidates, and selected by the trigger and event-level requirements. The efficiency of each step is defined as a conditional probability: the fraction of events that pass a given selection, given that they have passed all previous selections. Computing each efficiency step conditionally --- rather than cumulatively with respect to the generated sample --- isolates the effect of individual selection criteria and facilitates systematic studies.

The efficiency is evaluated using fully simulated MC samples (generator, detector simulation, and reconstruction). Because the kinematic distributions in simulation may differ from those in data --- particularly due to the unknown SPS-to-DPS mixture --- the efficiency is computed in the same $(p_{\mathrm{T}}, |y|)$ bins used for the acceptance. In each bin, the conditional efficiency is
\begin{equation}
\epsilon_i(\vec{x}) = \frac{N(\text{pass step } i \;\cap\; \text{pass steps } 1,\dots,i-1;\;\vec{x})}
                         {N(\text{pass steps } 1,\dots,i-1;\;\vec{x})},
\end{equation}
where $N$ denotes the number of events in the given kinematic bin. This bin-by-bin approach ensures that the correction factor does not depend on the assumed production kinematics of the MC generator.

The efficiency chain for this analysis is summarised in Table~\ref{tab:efficiency-chain}. For the HLT trigger efficiency, the denominator is the set of events in which both $J/\psi$ candidates and the $\phi$ candidate have been reconstructed and identified; a data-driven cross-check of the trigger efficiency will be performed as part of the closure tests.

\section{Candidate Choice and Migration Effects}
\label{sec:candidate-choice-migration}

In events with multiple $\phi$ mesons or multiple $K^+K^-$ combinations satisfying the selection criteria, more than one $J/\psi J/\psi \phi$ candidate can be formed. This analysis adopts a deterministic best-candidate rule: among all valid candidates in an event, the one that maximises
\begin{equation}
S(c) = \sqrt{\,p_{\mathrm{T}}^2(J/\psi_1) + p_{\mathrm{T}}^2(J/\psi_2) + p_{\mathrm{T}}^2(\phi)\,}
\end{equation}
is retained. The same best-candidate rule is applied at both the generator level and the reconstruction level, so that the definition of the fiducial measurement is internally consistent: the event is characterised by the candidate that would be selected by this rule, rather than by a predetermined ``hardest'' $\phi$ meson.

When the generator-level best candidate and the reconstruction-level best candidate involve different $\phi$ mesons or different $K^+K^-$ pairings --- a situation referred to as candidate mismatch --- the reconstructed kinematics may migrate from one bin to another with respect to the generator-level truth. The extent of this migration has been studied by constructing the generator-to-reconstruction response matrix for each differential variable. The response matrix is found to be approximately diagonal for the $(p_{\mathrm{T}}, |y|)$ variables of the individual mesons, with bin purities and stabilities above 90\% in most bins. A detailed discussion of the multi-candidate problem, the definition of the response matrix, and the validation of the bin-by-bin correction approach is given in Appendix~\ref{app:multicandidate}.

\section{Closure Tests}
\label{sec:closure-tests}

Each selected event is assigned a total correction weight
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:correction-weight}
w = \frac{1}{A_{\mathrm{tot}} \cdot \epsilon_{\mathrm{tot}}},
\end{equation}
where $A_{\mathrm{tot}}$ and $\epsilon_{\mathrm{tot}}$ are evaluated at the kinematic coordinates of the event using the acceptance and efficiency maps described above. This weight represents the expected number of produced events that each selected event stands for.

The acceptance and efficiency maps are computed from MC simulation. Because the relative contributions of the SPS and DPS production mechanisms are not known \textit{a priori}, the efficiency maps may depend on the assumed mixture. A closure test is performed to verify that the acceptance-and-efficiency correction reproduces the known generator-level event count when applied back to the MC sample:
\begin{equation}
N_{\mathrm{gen}} \;\overset{?}{\approx}\; \sum_{\text{selected events}} w .
\end{equation}
The test is carried out on pure SPS and pure DPS samples, representing the two kinematic extremes of the physical data, using efficiency maps derived from SPS, DPS, and mixed samples. For the acceptance correction, all strategies give consistent results within statistical uncertainties, consistent with the expectation that acceptance is purely geometric and independent of the production mechanism. For the efficiency correction, a mild dependence on the SPS-to-DPS mixture is observed in the HLT and vertexing efficiency maps, which is taken as a systematic uncertainty (Chapter~\ref{chap:systematics}). The nominal correction uses efficiency maps derived from a sample with SPS:DPS~=~1:2, which is chosen based on the closure test performance and preliminary indications of the DPS-dominated nature of this final state.

\section{Efficiency-Corrected Signal Yield and sPlot Distributions}
\label{sec:corrected-results}

Each selected event is assigned a total correction weight
\begin{equation}
w = \frac{1}{A_{\mathrm{tot}} \cdot \epsilon_{\mathrm{tot}}},
\end{equation}
where $A_{\mathrm{tot}}$ and $\epsilon_{\mathrm{tot}}$ are evaluated at the kinematic coordinates of the event using the acceptance and efficiency maps described in Sections~\ref{sec:acceptance-correction} and~\ref{sec:efficiency-correction}. This weight represents the expected number of produced events that each selected event stands for.

To separate the signal component from backgrounds in the kinematic distributions, the sPlot technique~\cite{Pivk:2004ty} is employed. Using the three-dimensional fit model described in Section~\ref{sec:signal-extraction}, per-event signal weights (sWeights) are computed for the $N_{sss}$ component. The sWeights are validated by verifying that the signal-weighted mass projections reproduce the fitted signal shapes. The signal-weighted mass projections are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:fit-projections}. Clear resonance peaks are visible in all three signal projections, while the background components are smooth.

For each event, the combined weight is the product of the correction weight and the sWeight:
\begin{equation}
w_{\mathrm{comb}} = w \times sWeight .
\end{equation}
Summing these combined weights over all selected events gives the efficiency-corrected inclusive signal yield:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{N_{sss}^{\mathrm{corr}} = \sum_{i} w_{i} \times sWeight_{i} .}
\end{equation}
